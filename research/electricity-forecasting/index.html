State-space models for electricity forecasting

Online prediction of multivariate electricity loads — two state-space models developed during my master's thesis at the Basque Center for Applied Mathematics.

Collaboration
Basque Center for Applied Mathematics (BCAM)
Status
Master's thesis · University of Trento
Year
2023

The problem

Electricity load forecasting matters: grid operators need short-horizon predictions to balance supply and demand in real time, and small improvements in accuracy translate into substantial savings and reliability gains. The forecasting problem is also genuinely multivariate — loads at different points on the grid are correlated, share daily and weekly seasonality, and respond jointly to weather and economic activity. Treating them independently throws away useful information.

Approach

For my master's thesis I developed two state-space models for online prediction of multivariate electricity loads. State-space models offer a natural way to combine latent dynamic structure (trend, seasonality, regime) with observation-level coupling between series, and they admit efficient recursive updates — important for the online setting where forecasts must be revised as new measurements arrive.

The work was carried out in collaboration with the Basque Center for Applied Mathematics in Bilbao, and was the bridge between my mathematics training in Trento and my current PhD work on network and complex-systems modelling.

Background

I received both my bachelor's and master's degrees in applied mathematics from the University of Trento, Italy. The thesis was the basis on which I joined CSH in April 2023 to pursue my PhD in network science.
